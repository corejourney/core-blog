My mother passed away exactly eight years ago on this exact date, July the second. Precisely in the midpoint of the year. Regretfully, I cannot make it home this year to visit her grave, but the thought of her is just enough for me right now. I wonder what she would think of the present me, still single and in between jobs. I think that she would grin and tell me, "It's okay. You'll bounce back in no time," and go back to the chores that she should have stopped doing years ago.

If I were to speak honestly, I don't think about my mother much anymore. Maybe three or four times per month, or just one time for especially stressful times. But with all the time that I have now, I have been seriously reevaluating everything. For the past week I have been taking aimless walks around the city, under the sunny sky, or walking through the rain. On this day it's cloudy, and it looks like it's about to rain.

My mother started to go on these trips after I turned 19. She would leave without warning at night, or in the morning, anytime where no one would notice her leaving. In her absense my father and I would think, "It's happened again..." and quietly wait for her return, which usually took around a day. She always did come back of course, in her usual cheerful self, but she would neither tell us where she was or what she did. This bothered me heavily for a reason I could not describe back then. In my head there were countless questions. Why does she do this and not tell us? Where exactly does she go? And why does she feel that she has to be gone alone? In my head I thought that it was because she felt the need to escape from some atrocity, but what was it exactly? I didn't know.

Mom, if you were here I would apologize to you. I know that you don't do well with tragedy or bad feelings. But it wasn't fair of me back then to expect you to know how I felt. To hold my problems so deep within me that even I couldn't describe why I was so depressed at times. If I had the courage to say those things out loud, it could have been okay so much faster. But you're gone now, those problems are gone now, and there's nothing left of it except this vague regret.

My mom called that she had something to tell me the day that she died. Her time was near. Both of us knew that for sure. I remember that on that day I got off work early to get to the hospital, just to find my mom weak and almost non-verbal. I begged her to tell me what she had to say in an almost frantic voice, but she was only able to close her eyes and shake her head, while squeezing my hands as tight as she could. It took all that was in me to resist crying to save her some grief. Shortly after around noon, she died without a word. I used to curse my bad luck for how that day played out, but I think now that in a way it was the only way she could have gone that would have made sense.

The clouds dissipate above me without leaving any rain, revealing the night sky. Squinting my eyes, I can make out a suggestion of an image from the top of this hill. I see my mom's car somewhere, those roads in my hometown, those faces, that turning and stillness with no result. In an instant, I turn and make for home. Today was a good day.
